# Fireproof — Submission

## 1. The industry, in your words

Fire and life safety ITM looks like one trade from the outside and is actually three jobs in a trench coat. A contractor like Beacon has to satisfy three audiences — AHJ, insurance carrier, and eventually counsel — using artifacts authored for none of them. The deepest structural fact is that compliance is not a single standard but a three-layer stack: NFPA publishes on multi-year revision cycles; each AHJ adopts a specific edition and overlays its own interpretations (some of which live only in a retired marshal's filing cabinet, à la Donnelly in the packet); and a carrier underwrites against its own loss-control criteria the AHJ never sees. The same building, on the same day, has three valid versions of "compliant." The incumbent software stack — BuildingReports, InspectPoint, and similar — flattens that reality into one PDF, because the routine 80% of the work fits on one PDF. The regulated 20% does not, and that is where the money and the lawsuits are.

The party with the most actual leverage is not the AHJ. It is the **insurance carrier**. The AHJ can issue a Notice of Violation; the carrier can non-renew a building that is 100% AHJ-compliant. Karen Fielding's Day -416 loss-control survey is the load-bearing document in the Cedar Heights file even though it has no enforcement weight — Recommendation 5.2-A on the aging fire pump and 5.5-A on retaining impairment records are technically advisory and functionally mandatory. Neither exists anywhere in Beacon's ITM software. There is nowhere to put them. They live in Karen's PDF, possibly forwarded to Linda once, and then decay.

The second structural fact, less polite, is that the contractor's pricing model creates a soft conflict of interest. Inspections are loss leaders; repairs are margin. The contractor identifying problems is the contractor profiting from the fix, which means the contractor also has the option of *not* identifying a problem when the customer has signaled they do not want to spend. That is the operational form of Tom's "let it sit" to Linda on Day -62 (Artifact 5). The 9th-floor west standpipe corrosion was known in the office on Day -78, kept out of the official report on Day -77, written as a proposal, ignored by the customer, and quietly carried into the next quarterly cycle with no decision recorded. There is no software field anywhere that captures "customer was offered a fix, did not respond, and has silently absorbed the risk." There is no field because nobody wants to write it.

Mike's Day -198 training transcript is the third tell. He writes the quarterly and the annual "the same way," does not call in under-four-hour impairments, and treats notification as "paperwork." This is not a Mike problem; it is a record-system problem. His heuristics are reasonable trade behavior in a world where the only artifact is the report PDF — and the report PDF is the artifact precisely because the software stack has nowhere else to put nuance. The Day -116 impairment ran 5h50m, exceeding Hartwell's 4-hour threshold, and Linda's "was AHJ notified on this one? Don't see anything in the file" sat for 116 days as `unknown` — a state invisible to every existing inspection product because none model it.

What the industry's software is missing, in one line: a stateful, evidence-grade record of the exceptional events — separated from the inspection PDF, durable enough to survive a multi-party records demand, and structured enough that an AHJ, a carrier, and a subrogation attorney can each pull what they need without contradicting each other.

## 2. The failure point

**The system has no stateful, evidence-grade exception ledger separate from the inspection report — so every nuance of the regulated 20% (impairments, customer-non-response deficiencies, carrier recommendations, asset-identity mismatches) decays into handwriting, technician memory, and one-line emails the moment it leaves the field.**

This is not "Beacon should have written it down better." Beacon did write it down. Mike logged the impairment on Day -116. Linda wrote her question to Mike about AHJ notification, and Mike wrote back with his 4-hour heuristic. The standpipe corrosion conversation is preserved across four emails between Day -78 and Day -47. Continental's 5.2-A and 5.5-A recommendations are in a PDF dated Day -416. The battery model number is on a manufacturer plate and on a different line in a Beacon ITM report. *None of this is missing.* What is missing is a system that knows any of these things are open, what evidence each one requires before it can be closed, and what closure even means.

That is why the post-incident cascade plays out the way it does. Mike's 5h50m impairment sits at `notification = unknown` for 116 days because no system is asking. Tom's "let it sit" becomes the final disposition of a known critical-path deficiency because the deficiency has no state — it is just an unanswered email. The Day -211 quarterly says `Deficiencies: None this cycle` while a parallel internal thread openly discusses corrosion on the same component, because the report and the email live in two universes that never reconcile. The carrier's pump-aging recommendation is alive in Continental's file and dead in Beacon's because nothing imported it as a tracked obligation. The battery identity mismatch survives years of paperwork because nobody compares structured asset claims across documents. On Day +12, when Reyes's NOV cites all five at once, Beacon discovers that all five were technically discoverable but none were actually tracked — by anyone, anywhere.

Any contractor running this way produces this outcome. The conflict of interest in the inspection-vs-repair revenue model (a defensible alternate failure point) makes it worse, but the records-integrity gap is upstream of it: even a contractor with no commercial incentive to suppress a deficiency would still lose this fight, because the system gives them no place to keep the truth. The records gap is also the failure point software can move first. You cannot legislate away the contractor's pricing model in five days. You can ship the ledger.

## 3. How your product or feature works

**Fireproof is an exception-operations ledger that sits above routine ITM software and turns each impairment, deficiency, carrier recommendation, and asset-identity mismatch into a stateful record with rule-driven evidence requirements that block invalid closure.** It is not an inspection-report authoring tool. It is the layer that catches everything the inspection report can't hold.

The core abstraction is the **exception**, in one of four types, each with its own state machine. An impairment moves Draft → Active → SafeguardsPending → RepairInProgress → RestorationTestingRequired → RestoredEvidenceIncomplete → ClosedAuditReady. The Day -116 impairment is seeded at `restored_evidence_incomplete` because the field record is exactly that: physically restored, administratively unfinished. Closure is not a button — it is a transition the API refuses (`BLOCKING_REQUIREMENTS_UNMET`) until every blocking evidence requirement is valid. That is the wedge.

Rules are versioned and jurisdiction-scoped, modeling the three-layer stack directly in the data. The MVP ships three packs: **Hartwell** (high-confidence; the Day -116 impairment fires `hartwell.impairment.gt4h.v1` and requires AHJ notification, fire-watch record, and a numeric main-drain restoration test), **Wessex** (medium; impairment-notification rule deliberately *unspecified* until verified, so we do not enforce a block we can't defend), and **Dunmoor** (low; NFPA 25-2020 inferred, all local amendments disabled pending review). That third pack is the honest one — Fireproof refuses to invent rules it doesn't have.

Evidence validation is load-bearing and deliberately strict. A main-drain restoration test does not pass on "pressure good." It requires numeric static and residual readings with units, a performer, and a timestamp. Mike's Day -116 note would fail validation. Notification proof distinguishes `sent`, `not_sent`, and `unknown` as first-class states, because the system has to give "I didn't notify the AHJ" a legitimate place to live — fabricated certainty is worse than a recorded gap.

Around the core sit four supporting capabilities. **Contradiction detection** runs deterministically over structured claims from each document version: same property + same component + conflicting status flags the standpipe omission; structured asset comparison catches the Eagle-Picher battery mismatch; performance-variance comparison catches the 18% pump churn drift between the on-file annual and the Day +6 independent test. **The packet builder** emits stakeholder-specific exports (AHJ NOV response, owner, insurer, counsel) with a `manifest.json` listing every included document version with its SHA-256 hash, plus a chain-of-custody receipt. **The immutable originals vault** is WORM-style: checksum at ingest, supersession creates a new version row, and an active legal hold physically refuses delete and overwrite. **The PWA** is offline-first so the field tech can capture readings, photos, and voice notes in a basement pump room and sync on reconnect.

What Fireproof deliberately does not do: it does not author routine ITM reports — incumbents still own that, and we read their output rather than replace it. It does not file with the AHJ on the contractor's behalf — the manifest is for humans to send. It does not resolve the contractor's repair-revenue conflict of interest — it makes the conflict's artifacts (proposal sent, no response, customer declined) visible, which is the most software can honestly do. The contradiction engine is rule-based, not generative; it will miss subtle conflicts, which is why the contradiction map is a review surface rather than a verdict.

Adoption difficulty number one is **Mike**. The product asks him to enter numeric readings instead of "pressure good" and to mark notification status explicitly. The wedge is twofold: mobile capture faster than his clipboard (camera-first, voice-to-text, prior-photo side-by-side), and `unknown` as a legitimate status. The current system forces him to fabricate certainty; Fireproof gives him the right to be honest and lets the office handle the conversation that follows. Adoption difficulty number two is **Tom**. Fireproof makes "let it sit" a recorded customer-decision state with a timestamp and a proposal reference. He may read that as a loss of deniability; on that axis it is. The frame that works is defense, not visibility: had the office recorded "proposal sent Day -77, customer non-response logged Day -68, risk acceptance pending" on `exc_def_9w_corr`, Beacon would walk into a subrogation deposition with the strongest contractor defense available. The product is selling Tom the file he wishes he had on Day +12.

## 4. Two honest answers

**What in the system comes close to covering this and why is your design better?**

The closest adjacent products are the routine ITM authoring tools — **BuildingReports** and **InspectPoint** — and they are genuinely good at the routine 80%: scheduled visits, NFPA-aligned templates, customer portals, deficiency lists on the report. Their impairment workflows, where they exist, are typically a checkbox on a report or a lightweight ticket, not a stateful object with rule-versioned evidence requirements and validated numeric readings. They do not separate the originals vault from the report. They do not run a contradiction engine across structured claims. They do not model jurisdiction-versioned rule packs, which is the only way to honestly model the three-layer stack. **iAuditor / SafetyCulture** are the generic mobile-inspection alternative — flexible, but not domain-aware and not built to survive a regulator demanding originals. **Box / SharePoint / DocuWare** can hold originals under retention policy but cannot enforce evidence completeness or rule-driven closure; they are passive filing cabinets.

Fireproof wins on the wedge because it is not trying to win the routine 80% — it is the exception ledger specifically. It accepts that a Beacon keeps authoring routine reports in their incumbent tool and bolts on at the side, ingesting those reports as one source of structured claims and treating impairments, carrier recommendations, non-response deficiencies, and asset-identity findings as the canonical work objects. The design premise is that originals, custody, and rule evaluation are the load-bearing artifacts when an AHJ, a carrier, and counsel demand records inside the same 96-hour window. That is the world this software was designed for; the incumbents were designed for the world before that demand arrives.

**Who in the system is incentivized to resist this and how do you address that?**

The most honest answer is **Tom Beacon**. Fireproof makes prior-period gaps visible — the absence of AHJ notification on a 5h50m impairment is no longer handwriting, it is a red `unknown` badge with a timestamp. That is uncomfortable for a contractor whose past records contain real ambiguity. The counter is not to deny the discomfort but to sell Tom the future Tom, not the past one: from go-live forward, every exception either closes audit-ready or stays open with its blockers visible, which is the strongest defense file a contractor can build under subrogation discovery. We pair that with two choices that lower the temperature — no required backfill of historical exceptions, and `unknown` as a legitimate first-class status so honest gaps can be recorded instead of papered over.

The second resistance, less political, is **Mike**. Thirty-one years in the trade, working heuristics, allergic to friction. The counter is mobile UX as the deliverable: the form has to be measurably faster than his clipboard. Camera-first capture, voice-to-text, prior-image side-by-side, and a wizard that auto-populates rule-required fields from system class and duration. If Fireproof is slower than paper, Mike loses and so do we. If it is faster, he adopts because it removes the office's follow-up calls he already hates.

The incumbents (BuildingReports, InspectPoint) will eventually bolt on an exception module. The defense is to ship the wedge first and build a friendly read-only integration with their reports — the more we depend on their output as a source of claims, the more switching cost we accumulate before they notice.

Worth saying explicitly: the party that, against surface intuition, **does not resist** is the carrier. Karen Fielding wants the manifest. Recommendation 5.5-A in the Day -416 survey literally asks the property manager to retain impairment records from the ITM contractor for redundancy. Fireproof is the productized version of that request. Carriers are the natural distribution channel.
